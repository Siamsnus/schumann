Reading the Charts

A practical guide to interpreting the live Schumann Resonance monitoring data. Each chart tells part of the story — together they paint a complete picture of Earth's electromagnetic environment.

The Spectrogram

The spectrogram is the centerpiece of Schumann monitoring. It's a heat map with three dimensions compressed into two:

Spectrogram axes

Horizontal axis (X): Time — typically 48 hours, running left (oldest) to right (newest). Timestamps are in Tomsk local time (UTC+7).

Vertical axis (Y): Frequency — from 0 Hz at top to 40 Hz at bottom. The Schumann modes appear as horizontal bands.

Color: Signal power — dark blue = low power, green/yellow = medium, red/white = high power.

What to look for

Clear, steady horizontal bands = normal conditions. Sudden brightening across all frequencies = geomagnetic disturbance. Bands disappearing or blurring = major ionospheric event. White vertical streaks = intense local or global lightning.

Amplitude Chart

Shows the signal strength of each Schumann mode over time. Each colored line represents one mode (1st through 5th). The amplitude is measured in picotesla (pT) — a tiny unit of magnetic field strength.

Normal vs. abnormal

Normal: Smooth daily cycle with amplitude peaking in afternoon (Tomsk time) when Asian/African thunderstorms are most active. The 1st mode is strongest, each subsequent mode is weaker.

Abnormal: Sudden sharp spikes or drops, modes changing relative strength, or loss of the normal daily rhythm. These often correlate with geomagnetic storms (check K-index).

Frequency Chart

Tracks the center frequency of each mode. Small fluctuations (±0.2 Hz) are normal. Larger shifts indicate significant changes in the Earth-ionosphere cavity.

Interpreting frequency shifts

Frequency increase: The effective ionosphere height has decreased (cavity is smaller). Common during solar flares when X-rays push the D-layer lower.

Frequency decrease: The effective ionosphere height has increased. Can occur at night or during ionospheric depletion events.

Sudden jump: A space weather event just changed the ionosphere. Check the solar X-ray flux for a corresponding flare.

Quality Factor (Q)

The Q-factor measures how "sharp" each resonance peak is. A high Q means the resonance is well-defined with a narrow peak. A low Q means the peak is broad and smeared.

Ionogram

An ionogram is produced by an ionosonde — a radar that transmits pulses at increasing frequencies straight up and measures what bounces back. The result shows the structure of the ionosphere above the station.

Reading the ionogram

X-axis: Transmitted frequency (MHz). Left to right = low to high.

Y-axis: Virtual height (km) — the apparent altitude from which the signal was reflected.

The trace: The colored curve shows reflection height vs. frequency. Each "layer" appears as a section where height gradually increases before a sharp jump (critical frequency reached).

Key layers: E-layer (~100 km), F1-layer (~200 km, daytime only), F2-layer (~250–400 km, strongest). The F2 critical frequency (foF2) is the most important parameter for Schumann cavity modeling.

K-Index & Geomagnetic Data

The K-index is a quasi-logarithmic scale (0–9) measuring geomagnetic field disturbance over 3-hour intervals.

Solar X-Ray Flux

The X-ray flux chart from GOES satellites is your early warning system. Solar flares produce bursts of X-rays that reach Earth in 8 minutes and immediately alter the ionosphere's D-layer.

Flare classification

A, B-class: Background level. No effect on Schumann data.

C-class: Small flare. Minor, usually undetectable effect.

M-class: Medium flare. Causes Sudden Ionospheric Disturbance (SID). Look for frequency shifts in Schumann data ~8 minutes after the flare peak.

X-class: Major flare. Significant ionospheric impact. May cause radio blackouts and clear Schumann anomalies.

Coronagraphs (LASCO C2/C3)

Coronagraphs show the solar corona by blocking the bright disk. Their primary purpose for Schumann monitoring is detecting coronal mass ejections (CMEs).

CME alert

If you see an expanding bright structure surrounding the entire occulting disk (a "halo CME"), Earth may be in the firing line. Impact: 1–3 days later, potentially causing a major geomagnetic storm visible in the K-index and Schumann data.

Putting It All Together

The charts tell a connected story. Here's a typical sequence during a solar event:

Timeline of a solar event

Hour 0: X-ray flux spikes (M or X-class flare detected by GOES).

Hour 0 + 8 min: Schumann frequencies shift as the D-layer is suddenly ionized. Amplitude may spike or drop.

Hours 1–6: Check LASCO for CME. If halo CME detected, prepare for geomagnetic impact.

Day 1–3: CME arrives. K-index jumps to 5+. Schumann amplitudes become erratic, Q-factors drop, frequencies shift significantly.

Day 3–5: Recovery. K-index returns to normal. Schumann patterns gradually return to their regular daily rhythm.
